Executive Summary

The instrument the field is missing.

Every major embodied AI program training robots for residential environments faces the same bottleneck: synthetic training data does not match real homes. The sim-to-real gap in residential spaces remains the primary barrier between laboratory demonstrations and reliable domestic deployment. Policies trained in simulation fail when confronted with the photometric complexity, spatial irregularity, and material diversity of actual houses.

EmbodiedNet is the instrument that measures this gap, the calibration layer between synthetic environments and real homes. Built over six years of continuous professional capture, it comprises 14-bit RAW HDR brackets, ego-perspective 4K video in cinema-grade log profiles, exterior stills, aerial footage, iPhone RGB-D walkthroughs, and metric floor plans across 500+ unique residential properties and 5,000+ distinct room-level scenes.

The corpus has never been licensed. It was captured by a single operator under a consistent methodology that matured alongside the equipment. The resulting kinematic consistency, provenance chain, and rights-clean title make it suitable for exclusive acquisition by a single entity. Patent pending.

The Synthetic Data Problem

Every lab builds in simulated homes.
No lab has real homes to measure against at scale.

Synthetic data generation is the prevailing approach to training embodied AI. Platforms like NVIDIA Isaac Sim, Meta AI Habitat, and Google's simulation tools generate massive volumes of virtual training data. The appeal is obvious: infinite scalability and full control. The problem is equally well-documented.

Photometric Divergence
Synthetic lighting distributions, material reflectances, and shadow behaviors differ systematically from real sensor captures.
Geometric Oversimplification
Idealized 3D meshes lack the surface irregularity, clutter density, and spatial variation of actual rooms.
Sensor Artifact Absence
Real cameras produce noise, lens distortion, depth quantization errors, and ambient light interference that simulators omit.
Distribution Shift
Object arrangements, room proportions, and material combinations in synthetic environments fail to match real residential distributions.

Why homes are uniquely difficult to simulate

Industrial environments have regularities that simulation can approximate: standardized racking, uniform lighting, predictable floors. Residential environments have none of these regularities.

A single home contains dozens of distinct material surfaces: hardwood transitioning to tile to carpet, granite adjacent to stainless steel, fabric next to glass. Natural light shifts throughout the day and across seasons, producing dynamic shadows and extreme luminance contrasts. Furniture arrangements are idiosyncratic. Rooms serve multiple overlapping functions.

Generating a synthetic residential environment at sufficient fidelity requires artists, 3D modelers, material scientists, and lighting engineers working for weeks per home. Generating 500 such environments is a project measured in years and tens of millions of dollars, with no guarantee of closing the transfer gap.

EmbodiedNet does not compete with simulation. It is the instrument that tells you whether your simulation is working. The real-world ground truth that synthetic pipelines are trying to approximate, captured at native sensor fidelity across 500+ unique residential environments and 5,000+ distinct scenes.

Structural Irreplaceability

Why this cannot be rebuilt

The critical question any prospective acquirer will ask is not whether this is good data. The question is: why can we not produce this ourselves? The answer operates across multiple independent dimensions, each a barrier that money alone cannot overcome.

Market Window Permanently Closed

The residential market between 2020 and 2023 was anomalous by any historical measure. Post-COVID demand migration, historically low mortgage rates, rapid new construction, and elevated agent marketing budgets drove transaction volume and listing density to levels not seen before or since. The economic environment that funded EmbodiedNet is a historical artifact.

Residential Access Cannot Be Recreated

The 500+ properties were privately owned homes. Access was granted under commercial photography agreements at time of listing. Those homes have since been sold. Owners have changed. Interior configurations have been modified. The specific combination of furnishings, staging, light conditions, and seasonal context no longer exists at any of these addresses.

Temporal Diversity Cannot Be Fast-Tracked

Four full seasonal cycles across six years means the corpus contains every seasonal lighting condition and six years of evolving design conventions: color trends, fixture styles, furnishing density, staging philosophies, and landscaping choices. A purpose-built capture sprint of any duration shorter than years produces a single seasonal slice.

Clean IP Chain of Title

All intellectual property is original to the collector. No data has been scraped, licensed from third parties, or generated synthetically. The corpus has never been licensed to any entity. This clean chain of title eliminates licensing encumbrance risks and enables exclusive acquisition structures that provide competitive advantage.

The Convergence

Three technical realities driving demand

Across independent labs, on different continents, using different methods, researchers are arriving at the same conclusion: data remains the most important component, and real-world data is still gold. Three trajectories converge on a single gap.

01
Human Data Scales. Environments Do Not.

Recent work demonstrates that human egocentric data scales robot performance with log-linear returns: pretraining on 20,000 hours of human demonstrations drives down action prediction error reliably as scale increases. Over 100 companies have launched in the past year to collect this data. But all of this scaling happens in a handful of lab environments. The demonstrations grow. The diversity of spaces they occur in does not. Policies trained on 20,000 hours in five rooms still fail in a sixth room they have never seen.

02
Real Scans Outperform Synthetic Training.

Synthetic data is infinite in principle but hard to scale in practice. Recent results from TU Munich show that models trained directly on incomplete real-world 3D scans outperform baselines trained on complete synthetic scenes. The reason is straightforward: real environments contain photometric complexity, material diversity, and spatial irregularity that procedural generation cannot replicate. The field is learning that real-world ground truth is not a supplement to simulation. It is the reference standard simulation is measured against.

03
Zero-Shot Transfer Degrades with Environment Diversity.

Researchers have demonstrated policies that deploy zero-shot into apartments the robot has never seen, using objects it has never trained on. The results are real. But they degrade as the gap between training environments and deployment environments widens. A policy trained in simulation on 100 synthetic apartments transfers reasonably to a test lab. It transfers less well to a real suburban kitchen with granite counters, warm light, and a layout no simulator has modeled. Closing this gap requires real residential data at scale.
